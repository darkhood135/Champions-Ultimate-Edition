🎯 The Basics

Champions Ultimate Edition is an RPG starring characters from various iconic series such as Marvel, DC, Dragon Ball and more. The center of the game is combat, customization and progression.

The battle system of Champions Ultimate Edition is layered with complexity (in movement, battle and concept). Previous versions of the Champions battle system were akin to a simple RPG like Marvel Strike Force. This has been completely overhauled. More movement mechanics and abilities are present now to create an experience more akin to Baldur's Gate 3. That's where the Ultimate Edition comes in.

🦸 Champions

Champions are the playable characters in Champions Ultimate Edition. They have a variety of roles, abilities, stats, tags, etc. that define them. Some characters have more utility than others, while some specialize in tanking or specific types of damage. The variety is abundant, and balancing a team with this in mind is crucial (see Team Building for more information).

The main aspects that make up a Champion are as follows:

Roles

A Champion's role defines the broad strokes of their gameplay archetype. Each Champion has exactly one role, which sets expectations for how they'll play. Specific behavior within a role — single-ally vs. team-wide buffs, melee vs. ranged emphasis, etc. — is refined further by tags.

The nine roles are organized into four categories:

⚔️Offensive

  • Striker: Single-target specialists. Primary focus is on eliminating one target at a time. Little to no utility or support. Often weak defensively with high damage.
  • Blaster: Multi-target specialists. Primary focus is on eliminating multiple targets at a time. Little to no utility or support. Often very weak defensively with high damage.
  • Berserker: High-risk, high-reward damage dealers. Trade their own HP, resources or defenses for massive damage spikes. Often have escalation mechanics — the worse off they are, the more dangerous they become. A reckless role that can win games when played correctly.

🛡️Defensive

  • Defender: The tanks of a team. Use high health, high defenses, taunts and damage redirects to focus fire onto themselves. Deal moderate damage with moderate utility.

💗Supportive

  • Healer: Restoration-focused. Keeps the team alive through healing, revival and cleansing. Often frail, but possibly the most important role a team can have.
  • Booster: Buff-focused. Improves ally stats and grants supportive effects. Some Boosters spread buffs across the whole team; others act as a "sidekick" focused on a single ally — that distinction is captured by tags.

🌀Disruptive

  • Breaker: Disrupts the enemy team with inflictions and debuffs. Seeks to mess up enemy strategy and harm enemy utility.
  • Summoner: Focuses on summoning allies to aid in battle. Typically doesn't deal much damage by themselves but ramps up while summons are involved. Allows for solid board control and focus shifts. Summoned allies can exceed the 6 Champion requirement and are controlled the same way as normal Champions.
  • Plunderer: A selfish role that harms allies and enemies alike to fuel themselves. Steals buffs, drains resources, and may even sacrifice teammates for personal power. Risky and abrasive, but capable of dominating in the right hands.

Stats

Stats are values that define a Champion's baseline capabilities. They can be modified temporarily by buffs and debuffs but are otherwise static. Every Champion has the following stats:

  • MAX HP: Maximum hit points. The maximum health value of a Champion. Can be exceeded with specific status effects.
  • ATK: Attack. The main stat utilized for physical damage. Ex. Deals 100% ATK damage.
  • MAG: Magic. The main stat utilized for ranged/magical damage. Ex. Deals 100% MAG damage.
  • HEAL: The main stat utilized for healing abilities. Ex. Restores 300% HEAL in HP.
  • DEF: Defense. The defensive stat utilized when damaged by an ATK move.
  • RES: Resistance. The defensive stat utilized when damaged by a MAG move.
  • ACC: Accuracy. Chance a move will successfully land. Only applies to moves which involve another character (healing, damaging, etc.).
  • CRIT: Healing/damage percentile which applies if a critical hit is rolled successfully with CRIT RATE.
  • CRIT RATE: Chance for a critical hit.
  • SPD: Decides movement order (higher SPD goes first).
  • EVA: Evasion. Chance to dodge an incoming attack.

Vitals

Vitals are character-specific values that govern resource pools and regeneration. They behave like stats but are tracked separately because they don't scale through upgrades and aren't subject to skill allocation.

  • MAX EN: Maximum energy. Can be exceeded with specific status effects.
  • MAX STA: Maximum stamina (the "furthest movement possible"). Can be exceeded with specific status effects.
  • REGEN: The percentage of HP restored once a turn begins. Varies significantly between Champions — a Wolverine regenerates significantly more than a Bullseye.

Every Champion also has default regeneration rates that apply at the start of their turn:

  • EN regen: 20% of MAX EN per turn
  • STA regen: 40% of MAX STA per turn
  • ULT gain: 5% per turn

These defaults can be modified — or nullified entirely — by passives, status effects or kit-specific notes (e.g., "cannot gain ULT passively"). Most ULT charge typically comes from a Champion's actions, not the passive 5%.

Resources

Resources are similar to stats but change dynamically throughout the fight. Stats only change when modified by an effect; resources are constantly in flux. Every Champion has the following resources:

  • HP: Hit points. The health of the Champion. When reduced to zero, the Champion is defeated and removed from battle (with exceptions of endurance or resurrection).
  • EN: Energy. Consumed for special abilities.
  • STA: Stamina. Consumed for mobility.
  • ULT: Ultimate charge. Acts as a percentile. When at 100%, ULTIMATUM (the ultimate ability) can be utilized. Charges in various ways depending on the Champion.

Unlike stats and vitals (which all Champions share), some Champions have extra resources unique to themselves. Examples include KI, CLUES, etc.

Items

Items are (usually) found throughout the battlefield and can provide various effects. Each Champion can hold up to 8 unique items at a time with a per-item limit of 3 (though it's incredibly rare that either limit will be reached). Essentially, each Champion has their own inventory. Inventories start empty unless specified by a passive, artifact, etc.

Some Champions generate their own items under certain conditions. For example, some characters need ammo they can acquire by performing certain actions or movements.

Some items are non-consumable and simply remain in the inventory providing an effect (almost like an extra passive ability). In order to use a consumable item, a Champion must use their "Item" basic ability during their turn.

Items can be discarded freely. If an inventory is full, the player must discard an existing item to make room for a new pickup.

Links and Tags

All Champions have links and tags which define what teams they'll work best on. They function similarly in execution and concept.

Links define their personality, relationships and motivations. Examples include morality (Hero, Villain, Anti-Hero, etc.), teams (Avengers, Justice League, etc.) and mindset (Soldier of War, Road to Redemption, Pure Heart, etc.).

Tags showcase how the Champion behaves in gameplay and allow players to narrow down Champions for skills they deem most valuable. For example, a Champion who counters could have the "Quick Counter" tag. Tags also handle the sub-distinctions inside a role — a Booster might have a "Single-Target Buff" tag to clarify their sidekick-style playstyle.

There is no limit to the amount of tags or links a Champion can have.

Leader Ability

Every Champion has one leader ability (essentially another passive, often providing a stat buff for the team) which only applies when they're the leader. Each team can only have one leader.

Most leader skills only apply to specific links, so focusing a team around 1-3 links is often the best choice. Many Champion skills trigger (or apply) when other Champions of a specific link are present, so keeping an eye on synergy and Champion kits is crucial when creating a team.

Basic Abilities

Basic abilities encompass 7 major actions, 1 of which is unique to each Champion.

  • Unique Basic Ability
  • Item
  • Use
  • Block
  • Wait
  • Move
  • End Turn

The unique basic ability is an ability unique to each Champion. It's essentially a special ability that doesn't require EN or have a cooldown. It typically deals less damage and is less effective but won't cost resources (allowing it to be used in tricky positions or when being conservative). Like a special ability, it has an effective range. This ability can only be used once per turn.

The Item action lets a Champion consume a consumable from their inventory. It's the only way to actively trigger an item's effect during a turn. Not all consumable items work the same way — two broad categories exist:

  • Consumable items (e.g., Medkit) target the Champion themselves. They require no range check, no line-of-sight, and are not subject to ACC or EVA rolls — their effect resolves automatically on use.
  • Throwable items (e.g., Lantern, Firework) are aimed at a target panel within the item's listed range. They follow standard line-of-sight rules for targeting. The throw itself is not subject to ACC or EVA rolls — it lands on the chosen panel regardless. Any effects the item then applies to Champions on or around that panel (damage, status inflictions, etc.) follow normal ability calculation rules, including EVA rolls for affected targets.

The Use action can be activated when a Champion is adjacent to an interactable object on the battlefield — a computer terminal, a button, a lever, or similar — that carries a triggered special effect. Activating it resolves the object's effect as described by the battlefield or mission context. Not every obstacle or object is interactable; only those explicitly flagged as such will respond to this action.

Blocking causes the Champion to be inactive until their next turn. While blocking, the Champion's DEF and RES are multiplied by 1.5x for incoming damage. There is danger, however: some abilities can break a Champion's block for the turn (called Piercing abilities). Piercing abilities are automatic critical hits and ignore the block multiplier when hitting a blocking target. Blocking can only be chosen as the first ability used in the turn and will end the turn for that Champion.

Waiting causes the Champion to be inactive until their next turn with the benefit that, on the next turn, the Champion gains 1.5x the EN, STA and HP regeneration they would normally. The block multiplier does not apply while waiting — only while blocking. Waiting can only be chosen as the first ability used in the turn and will end the turn for that Champion. Some kits trigger effects when the Champion Waits — for example, Deadpool's core passive increases Trickshot's cooldown by 3 turns each time he Waits — so Waiting is a hookable action, not only a regenerate-and-skip button.

Moving allows the Champion to move where it wishes (assuming they have the STA) without committing to an action. Consumes 1 STA per panel in the battlefield's grid. This ability won't cause a Champion's turn to end — another ability can be used immediately after. Elevation, gap-jumping, floor traversal and void interaction all affect how movement resolves — see Elevation, Gap Jumping, Floors, and Void and Falling in the Battle System section for the full rules.

Ending the turn will end your Champion's actions for the turn (acting as the "finished" button).

Character sheets do not list Block, Wait, Use, Move and End Turn, but all Champions can use them at any time unless specified otherwise by a passive or status effect.

Special Abilities

Special abilities are the core abilities of a Champion. Each Champion has 6 special abilities which can be interchanged, with only 4 being chosen to use in battle. There is no "core" (required) special ability — they can all be interchanged (unlike passives where there is one core passive). These abilities are selected when teambuilding, not in battle. Whatever abilities are chosen before the battle will carry over and can't be changed in the middle of battle.

Every special ability utilizes EN and has a resource unique to itself called a cooldown. A cooldown is the amount of turns before it can be used again. If a Champion still has a cooldown on a special ability (or doesn't have enough EN), the action cannot be used. Multiple special abilities can be used in the same turn if the Champion has enough energy.

Likewise, every special ability has a range (almost like its "line of sight"). Since a battlefield consists of a shaped setup (divided into squares like a grid, see the battle system section), a Champion can only perform an action on another if they're within that many panels of their target. Range is also affected by obstacles, which get in the way. Champions who have enough STA to reach their target will path-find (consuming the least STA possible) into an effective, valid range. Basic abilities also utilize the range mechanic (when relevant).

Special abilities are divided into subtypes:

  • Melee — typically have short ranges and cannot affect flying/airborne targets.
  • Ranged — typically have long ranges and can affect flying/airborne targets.
  • Support — focus on providing support to their target (whether self or another ally).
  • Transformation — transform the Champion into another form. This form will often have an entirely unique moveset to their base form. Abilities do not carry over between forms (unless they share abilities pre and post transformation, in which case the cooldowns are carried over). Status effects and resources carry over between forms.

Passive Abilities

Passive abilities are abilities that are activated passively (duh). Some of them are permanently and always active, while others require a condition to be fulfilled. Each Champion has 1 guaranteed passive ability (the core passive) and 4 other passive abilities which can be interchanged (with only 2 being chosen to use in battle, 3 total including the core). These abilities are selected when teambuilding, not in battle.

Passive abilities can range from simple stat buffs, to new strengths and playstyles, to automatic actions (like counters), to overrides of default vitals (e.g., "EN regen is doubled; ULT cannot be gained passively"), to status-resistance effects for Champions who specialize in shrugging off inflictions.

ULTIMATUM

ULTIMATUM, also known as an ultimate ability, is a massive and powerful ability that relies upon the ULT resource. Once ULT reaches 100% for a Champion, they can use their ULTIMATUM. These abilities act as incredibly strong special abilities. As such, they come in many forms (melee, ranged, etc.). Some may be single-target, others may be multi-target.

ULTIMATUM costs no energy and has no cooldown. Once ULTIMATUM is used, ULT is reduced to 0% (unless specified otherwise by a passive or status effect).

Synergies

Synergies are bond-based abilities a Champion gains only while a specific partner Champion is present on the team. Each synergy represents the relationship between two characters — a friendship, a rivalry, a shared history — and expresses that bond mechanically by granting the Champion new or overhauled abilities that exist only while the partner is fielded.

Most bonds are mutual — both Champions have a synergy with each other and both gain something — but this is not required. Sometimes only one side benefits, and sometimes one side benefits far more than the other. A bond that is strong enough may grant brand-new combo potential between the two kits. Cross-franchise synergies are valid — the main story includes relationships that span universes and may at first seem strange.

Because synergies are team-based rather than build-based, they can exceed a Champion's normal ability limits: a synergy-granted special or passive is added on top of the standard loadout (surpassing the passive and 4-special roadblocks) and does not occupy a build slot. There is no limit to the number of synergies a Champion can have.

A synergy grants one or more abilities — almost always one, occasionally two — drawn from the following types:

  • New Passive — grants the Champion a new passive, usually one or two lines in the style of a standard passive (stat buffs, conditional triggers, and similar).
  • New Special — grants the Champion a brand-new special ability. Added beyond the normal 4-special limit; it is team-based, so it never displaces an equipped special.
  • Upgraded Special — overrides an existing special ability with a stronger new form that otherwise functions like a normal special. The overridden special must be in the active loadout to be used this way; if that special is not equipped, the upgrade cannot be accessed.
  • Upgraded ULTIMATUM — overrides an existing ULTIMATUM with a stronger new form. As with Upgraded Specials, the overridden ULTIMATUM must be in the active loadout to be used.
  • Dynamic Special — a combo that requires both Champions to opt in, consuming resources from each on activation (one side may pay more than the other). Both must be able to act for the combo to fire; the granting kit specifies the shared targeting, range, and cost.

When two different synergies would upgrade the same base ability into different new forms, the Champion who owns that ability may choose at will which upgrade to use each time they use the ability during the match. Neither synergy overrides the other, and both remain usable — so a team fielding two partners that each modify one ability keeps the full value of both bonds.

When a synergy Upgrades (overrides) a base ability into a new form, that upgraded form inherits the base ability's identity for all rule references. Any other kit line — a passive, costume, synergy, leader ability, and so on — that names the base ability continues to apply to the upgraded form while the override is active. For example, The Wasp's Surge Model II reduces the cooldown and healing of Stinging Surge. While Stinging Surge is overridden into Starry Surge by the Soul of the Avengers synergy, Surge Model II's modifiers still apply to Starry Surge — so it would still take the cooldown reduction, the healing reduction, and the extra EN loss.

Upgrades

Every Champion has an upgrade system behind all of their stats and abilities. Character sheets account for Champions at their max power (10 star, level 150, maxed out skill tree) but without the expanded skill tree (so custom stat allocations are not accounted for).

The stats upgraded by levels, stars and skill allocation are:

  • MAX HP
  • ATK
  • MAG
  • HEAL
  • DEF
  • RES
  • SPD

Stats that do not upgrade via levels, stars or skill allocation:

  • ACC
  • CRIT
  • CRIT RATE
  • EVA

All Vitals (MAX EN, MAX STA, REGEN) are also static — changes to these come from passives, modifiers and status effects rather than upgrades.

Boosts apply in the following order:

Raw stats → Level multiplier → Star multiplier → Skill allocation multiplier → Leader ability multiplier → In-game multipliers (passives, status effects, etc., all additive)

Modifiers

Modifiers are extra passives, usually stat buffs. They are only obtained from costume effects and artifacts. Since modifiers are extra passives, their increases multiply on top of skill allocation.

Costume Effects

Costume effects (also known as costume buffs) are extra passives that come attached with certain outfits (usually stat boosts). These effects have unique names and can be utilized without using the skin they came with. For example, upon unlocking a Symbiote skin for Spider-Man, the player would receive:

  • Symbiote Outfit
  • "Alien Awareness"

"Alien Awareness" became unlocked with the Symbiote Outfit, but it can be used on any other skin. By default, only one costume effect can be active at a time. Default skins do not have costume effects.

Level 150 endgame unlock Reaching level 150 with a Champion permanently grants them a second costume effect slot — the only way to slot two costume effects simultaneously. Alongside this, the level 150 unlock includes a unique exclusive costume and a powerful new costume effect tied to it. This is the headline endgame reward for maxing a Champion.

Artifacts

Artifacts are unlocked as rewards from various modes. They typically have equip conditions referencing a specific link or tag. If an artifact's conditions fit a Champion then that artifact can be equipped onto that Champion. Similar to costume effects, artifacts provide an extra passive to their user.

Only one artifact can be equipped at a time.

👥 Team Building

In normal battles, each team can have up to six Champions. These Champions have varying abilities, movesets, synergies, etc. which can be used in battle. Bringing Champions of various roles is important for variety and effective play.

Each team has one leader which grants the team bonus stats (or even an ability) based on the character's Leader Ability. Every Champion has a leader ability but only one can be active at a time. Choose this leader ability wisely.

Every player has 50 team slots (allowing for a significant amount of different teams)! Choices made regarding Champion abilities, skill allocations, artifacts and modifiers stay within a party so they don't have to be changed whenever they're switched to a different team.

Champions can each be customized in-depth to allow for unique mixes and strengths. Ensuring each Champion has an artifact, a costume effect and their skill allocation maxed out is crucial for success in difficult battles.

⚔️ The Battle System

All battles utilize teams, with Champions, on a battlefield. Battlefields are stages with differing items, obstacles, themes and elevations.

Standard Battles

The new battle system that makes Champions Ultimate Edition brand new.

At the start of a battle, characters start with maximum EN, STA and HP. Their ULT starts at 0%, with 0 items. Their abilities will start with a cooldown of their normal cooldown minus two turns, minimum 0 — so a 1-cooldown ability begins immediately usable, while a 5-cooldown ability begins with 3 turns remaining. These conditions can change if indicated otherwise by their kit.

Each Champion acts one at a time. Champions will act in order according to their SPD stat, with the highest SPD going first. Once all characters have acted, the cycle continues and a new turn begins for each Champion. If a Champion dies before their action can occur, their action is nullified.

When a Champion is about to act, they regenerate EN (default 20% of MAX EN), STA (default 40% of MAX STA), and HP (per their REGEN stat). They also gain ULT (default 5%). All of these defaults can be modified by passives.

While selecting abilities, each Champion can act as many times as they have the resources for and can choose who their ability targets per ability.

If a Champion has 100 EN, with two special abilities that each cost 50 EN, they can use both abilities in the same turn (assuming they're both off cooldown). They can also choose to save it for a future turn.

The most complicated aspect of standard battles is the movement system (described in the next section). Since characters can move around the battlefield, new aspects come into play.

The last team alive wins. If both teams die due to a self-destruction, the team who caused it wins.

Movement and the Battlefield

All Champions have access to the "Move" basic ability at all times (unless indicated otherwise by a passive or status effect). A Champion moving consumes 1 STA per panel in the battlefield's grid. This ability won't cause a Champion's turn to end — another ability can be used immediately after. If a Champion runs out of stamina, they won't be able to move.

A battlefield consists of a shaped setup (divided into squares like a grid). A Champion can only perform an action on another if they're within the range (that many panels of their target) of their ability. Range is also affected by obstacles, which get in the way. Champions who have enough STA to reach their target will path-find (consuming the least STA possible) into an effective, valid range. Basic abilities also utilize the range mechanic (when relevant).

Elevation

When using the Move action, a Champion can always descend to a lower elevation freely. Ascending to a higher elevation requires a valid access point built into the battlefield — stairs, a ladder, an escalator, or a similar structure. If no such access point exists along the path, upward movement is not possible.

Two conditions override this restriction and allow free ascent regardless of the terrain: the Champion has a passive that explicitly states something along the lines of "ignores elevation when moving," or the Champion is currently airborne due to an active ability. In either case the elevation change carries no special access requirement.

Descent STA cost: Dropping exactly 1 elevation (−1) carries no extra STA cost — only the normal 1 STA per panel applies. Dropping more than 1 elevation at a time costs an additional 2 STA for each floor dropped beyond the first. A 3-floor drop, for example, costs 4 extra STA (2 floors beyond the first × 2 STA each). This extra cost can bring a Champion to exactly 0 STA and the descent is still permitted even if it would do so — a Champion entering a 3-floor drop with only 3 STA will reach the bottom at 0 STA. Airborne Champions and those who ignore elevation do not incur this extra STA cost when descending.

Visual note: An airborne Champion is displayed 1 elevation level above their ground panel. This is a visual representation only and does not affect range calculations or targeting rules.

Standable half-obstacles: Some half-obstacles explicitly permit a Champion to stand on top of them (e.g., the Brick Parapet). Standing on such an obstacle does not change the Champion's elevation tier — they remain at the same elevation as the floor beneath the obstacle. Half-obstacles provide no elevation advantage and do not affect line-of-sight or range calculations differently from a normal floor panel at that elevation.

Gap Jumping

A Champion can jump across a void gap of 1 or 2 panels wide at the same elevation, or across such a gap while descending to a lower elevation. The cost is the normal 1 STA to arrive at the destination panel plus 2 STA per gap panel crossed. Crossing a 1-panel gap costs 3 STA total; a 2-panel gap costs 5 STA total. The gap panels themselves are not counted toward normal movement distance — only the destination panel and the gap surcharge.

Unlike descending (which is permitted even if the cost would bring STA to 0), a gap jump cannot be attempted if the Champion does not have enough STA to cover the full cost. The jump is simply unavailable until sufficient STA is available. Gaps wider than 2 panels cannot be crossed by jumping — an ability that repositions or launches the Champion would be required instead.

Airborne Champions: The gap-jumping STA surcharge (2 STA per gap panel) still applies to airborne Champions. Being airborne removes the access-point restriction for ascending and the extra STA cost for descending, but it does not waive the gap-jump surcharge — the surcharge represents the lateral distance crossed, not a vertical restriction.

Floors

Floors are distinct layers of a battlefield, entirely separate from elevation. Where elevation describes height differences within a single connected field, a floor is a fully enclosed layer that can only be accessed through specific traversal points built into the map — an elevator, teleporter, staircase, ladder, or similar mechanism explicitly placed there. The number of floors a battlefield can have has no fixed limit.

Champions on different floors cannot interact with each other in any way — no attacking, no targeting with support abilities, and no triggering of passive effects across the boundary. For the duration of their time on a given floor, each floor is effectively its own isolated field of battle.

Void and Falling

Panels at the edge of a battlefield may border void — empty space off the map. Being pushed toward the void does not always result in a fall:

  • If a Champion is pushed into the void but was not standing on an edge panel beforehand, they catch themselves in time and land on the edge panel closest to whoever pushed them — the last valid tile before the void. No damage is taken.
  • If a Champion is already standing on an edge panel and is then pushed into the void, they fall. Any playable Champion recovers from the fall, taking 400 damage in the process and returning to the battlefield on the panel just inside the edge (one tile back onto the stage). Non-playable enemies such as minions are defeated outright by a fall with no recovery.

The 400 damage applies only to a full fall — being pushed to an edge panel carries no HP cost on its own.

Panel Occupancy

Two Champions cannot occupy the same panel simultaneously. If an ability would cause a Champion to land on a panel already occupied by another Champion, HP determines priority:

  • If the originally occupying Champion has less HP than the arriving Champion, the original is displaced — they are moved one panel in the direction from which the arriving Champion came. If that direction leads into a void, they are moved one panel to a valid adjacent side instead.
  • If the originally occupying Champion has more HP, or an equal amount, the arriving Champion is stopped one panel short of their destination.

A Champion and an obstacle likewise cannot share a panel. If an ability would place a Champion on a panel occupied by an obstacle, the Champion lands on the nearest adjacent valid panel instead.

Obstacle States

Obstacles do not remain in their original state indefinitely once interacted with:

  • Destroyed obstacles: When a destructible obstacle is reduced to 0 HP (or destroyed by any other means), it is removed from the battlefield entirely. Its panel becomes a normal, passable floor panel for the remainder of the battle. Any Champions that were displaced around it may now move through that panel freely.
  • Triggered interactives: When an interactive obstacle resolves its effect (e.g., a Pigeon Coop releasing pigeons, a crate dropping its item), it reverts to the non-interactive obstacle type defined in the battlefield entry — typically a half-obstacle or full obstacle that no longer responds to the Use action. The panel is still occupied by an obstacle; it simply cannot be activated again.

If a battlefield entry does not specify what an obstacle becomes after destruction or trigger, assume destroyed obstacles leave a clear floor panel and triggered interactives become inert full obstacles.

Range Falloff

Long-range abilities have an optimal range beyond which their accuracy decreases. The rules:

  • Abilities with a range of 9 panels or less have no falloff — their entire range is optimal.
  • Abilities with a range greater than 9 panels have an optimal range equal to 80% of their total range (rounded down). Within optimal range, accuracy is unaffected. Beyond optimal range, ACC is reduced by 2% per panel.

The falloff penalty is multiplicative with other ACC modifiers. For example, a long-range ability passing through a half-obstacle (−50% ACC) and fired beyond optimal range (−6% ACC) lands at 0.5 × 0.94 = 0.47 of its base accuracy, not 0.5 − 0.06.

This creates a meaningful tradeoff for snipers and long-range Blasters: a Champion can fire at maximum range, but is rewarded for closing into optimal range first.

Line Abilities

Some abilities target all enemies in a 3-panel-wide line extending from the user in a chosen direction. The beam begins on the panel immediately adjacent to the user and extends outward — the user's own panel is never included. All 8 compass directions are valid.

  • Cardinal directions (N, S, E, W): The 3-panel width covers the central direction and one panel to each side at every step outward. A northward beam at range 1 covers the N, NW, and NE panels adjacent to the user.
  • Diagonal directions (NW, NE, SW, SE): The width is achieved by including both flanking cardinal panels at each step. A NW beam at range 1 covers the N, NW, and W panels adjacent to the user, with each subsequent step following the same staircase pattern.

Champions caught anywhere within the swept area are affected, subject to normal line-of-sight and the standard ability calculation rules for each target.

Clash Battles

A recreation of the battle system used in Champions I-III.

At the start of a battle, characters start with maximum EN and HP. Their ULT starts at 0%, with 0 items. Their abilities will start with a cooldown of their normal cooldown minus two turns, minimum 0. These conditions can change if indicated otherwise by their kit.

Clash battles function on a two-phase system: Preparation and Execution. Preparation begins first, where both sides select their actions for all of their Champions. During this time, Champions on both sides regenerate EN and HP per their defaults. There is no STA or STA regen, meaning there is no movement limit.

While selecting abilities, each Champion can only act once per turn. Characters can choose who their ability targets manually. Once all actions have been selected, the turn enters the Execution phase.

In the execution phase, Champions act in order according to their SPD stat, with the highest SPD going first. Once all characters have moved, the cycle continues and a new turn begins. If a character dies before their action can occur, their action is nullified.

Battlefields remain the same in this mode but distance, obstacles and range falloff become nearly irrelevant.

The last team alive wins. If both teams die due to a self-destruction, the team who caused it wins.

🧮 Ability Calculations

The order for attack calculations is detailed as follows:

  1. Baseline damage. Calculate the percentage damage of the move using the relevant stat. Example: a 200% ATK move with 550 ATK = 1,100 baseline damage. Any buffs/debuffs applied to the source stat (leader skills, passives, etc.) are already baked in here.
  2. Critical roll. CRIT RATE is rolled. If successful, baseline damage is multiplied by the CRIT percentage.
  3. Infliction roll (if relevant). The attack rolls for its chance to inflict any negative status effects. Status resistance is not a stat — Champions who resist inflictions do so through specific passives.
  4. Accuracy and evasion. The defender rolls for EVA; the attacker rolls for ACC. If the defender's EVA roll is successful or the attacker's ACC roll fails, the attack is dodged.
  5. Block multiplier (if relevant). If the defender is blocking, multiply their relevant defensive stat (DEF for ATK damage, RES for MAG damage) by 1.5x.
  6. Defense subtraction. Subtract the defender's DEF (for ATK damage) or RES (for MAG damage) from the damage number.
  7. Situational damage modifiers. Apply any passive or status-effect-based damage modifiers (e.g., "takes 50% less fire damage"). These are additive when stacked.
Attack Calculation Order Baseline percentage damage → Critical roll/multiplier → Infliction roll (if relevant) → ACC + EVA rolls → Block multiplier on DEF/RES (if blocking) → Subtract defensive stat → Apply situational damage modifiers

Damage over time effects (Bleed, Shock, Burn, Frost, and equivalent DoT inflictions) deal their flat damage at the start of each affected turn and do not enter the attack calculation pipeline. They skip steps 2–7 entirely — no CRIT roll, no ACC/EVA roll, no block multiplier, and no defensive stat subtraction. DoT damage applies its flat value directly to HP. Any ability that deviates from this (e.g., a DoT that does scale with DEF) will say so explicitly in its description.

Multi-hit abilities: When an ability deals damage in multiple separate hits (e.g., "deals 200% ATK damage to the target 3 times"), the defender's DEF (or RES) subtraction in step 6 is applied independently to each hit. A 200% ATK × 3-hit move against a target with DEF 140 subtracts 140 three times — once per hit — rather than once for the whole ability. Abilities that describe a single combined damage total with no "times" qualifier apply the subtraction once as normal.

Area-of-Effect abilities: AoE abilities affect all valid targets within the defined area. Unless the ability explicitly states otherwise, AoE only affects enemies — allied Champions within the radius are unaffected. Each target within the area is resolved as a separate instance of the attack following the standard calculation order above. Specifically:

  • CRIT roll: Rolled once per target. One target may take a critical hit while another does not.
  • Infliction roll: Rolled once per target. Each target has an independent chance to receive any status effect.
  • ACC and EVA rolls: Rolled once per target. Each target rolls EVA and the attacker rolls ACC separately; one target may evade while another does not.

The result is that AoE abilities can produce different outcomes for each Champion in the area — some may be hit and inflicted, others may dodge entirely. Any ability that deviates from this (e.g., a guaranteed hit to all targets, or a single CRIT roll shared across the area) will say so explicitly.

Mixed ATK + MAG abilities: Abilities described as "Deals X% ATK and Y% MAG" in a single use are treated as two independent damage instances resolved in sequence. A single ACC/EVA roll determines whether the ability lands — if it misses, both instances miss. If it lands, each instance is calculated separately against its respective defensive stat: the ATK instance subtracts the target's DEF, and the MAG instance subtracts the target's RES. Each instance rolls for CRIT independently.

HP threshold scaling: Some abilities scale based on exact HP thresholds, typically phrased as "per X% of HP lost" or "per each X% HP threshold crossed." These trigger once each time the specified threshold is crossed during damage calculation. Continuous effects (Bleed, Regeneration, etc.) that bring HP to or through a threshold value trigger the threshold at the point it is crossed — not retroactively for HP states that occurred before that effect resolved.

When a Champion's current HP falls between two thresholds, use the floor rule: apply the highest complete threshold already passed, not the next one above. With 10% increments, a Champion at 85% HP has crossed 8 complete thresholds (the 80% mark), so the 80% threshold is active — the 90% threshold has not been reached and does not count. This interpretation applies consistently to all kit descriptions that use exact HP threshold language, regardless of which Champion uses it.

Healing works similarly but skips defenses and evasion:

  1. Calculate percentage healing
  2. Roll for critical
  3. Roll for application (if relevant)
  4. Roll for accuracy
  5. Apply target's healing modifier
Healing Calculation Order Baseline percentage healing → Critical roll/multiplier → Application roll (if relevant) → ACC roll → Apply target's healing modifier

Some exceptions could always apply (such as attacks ignoring defense altogether, guaranteed hits, guaranteed inflictions, immunity nullification, etc.). Any such exceptions will be made clear within an ability's description.

📐 Stat Calculations

Stats have a priority during calculations, similarly to ability calculations.

Stat Calculation Order Raw stats → Level multiplier → Star multiplier → Skill allocation multiplier → Leader ability multiplier → In-game multipliers (passives, status effects, etc., all additive)

An example would be with a raw stat of 500, a level increase of 20%, a star increase of 8%, a skill allocation increase of 2% (not possible by normal means with this level), a leader ability increase of 8%, a passive increase of 20% and a status effect increase of 5%.

  • 500 × 120% = 600
  • 600 × 108% = 648
  • 648 × 102% = 660 (round down)
  • 660 × 108% = 712 (round down)
  • 712 × 125% = 890

Stats round down to whole numbers in-game.

🎮 Content Types

Champions Ultimate Edition improves upon its predecessors by introducing more modes and ways to play.

Main Story

The main story of Champions Ultimate Edition. Tells a brand new and multiversal story of epic proportions directly tying into the previous. Also includes revamped and retconned stories from Champions I, II and III with revised story beats and improved missions. This mode unlocks many of the playable Champions and is the main source of early-game progression. Navigating the hubs between battles allows for resource gathering and plot development. Battles here are usually standard, but sometimes involve waves of enemies or unique bosses.

Event Story

Event stories are other campaigns (often limited-time) with new characters, artifacts, etc. Events tend to be a lot harder than the campaign but offer greater and more up-to-date rewards. Maintains the same gameplay style as the main campaign, offering explorable hubs and unique missions.

Arena

The main PvP gamemode utilizing the standard battle system. Two types of the Arena gamemode are available:

  • Sparring Session — simply for fun. It can still provide XP but serves primarily for casual play. Friends can compete against each other in this mode and all stars/levels are equalized. Each player is given 60 seconds to make their actions (excluding action animations) per Champion per turn.
  • Death Battle — incredibly competitive, with a ranked system. Items found throughout the battlefield are no longer randomized and the battlefield pool (maps able to be played on) is shrunk to ensure balance. The top players in Death Battle receive huge rewards every season (with each season lasting a month). Provides a lot of XP and rewards upon victory. Friends can still compete against each other in this mode but there will be no rewards. Each player is given 30 seconds to make their actions (excluding action animations) per Champion per turn.

Clash

A PvP mode which utilizes the old preparation/execution phase mechanics. This changes game flow entirely by requiring each side to choose all of their actions at once for the turn (rather than when the character is ready to act). On top of this, each Champion can only use one ability per turn (not including extensions). Stamina is disabled entirely in this mode (allowing Champions to move around the battlefield without limit), making it more reminiscent of the old style of Champions combat. Two types of the Clash gamemode are available:

  • Casual Clash — simply for fun. It can still provide XP but serves primarily for casual play. Friends can compete against each other in this mode and all stars/levels are equalized. Each player is given 120 seconds to make all of their actions per turn.
  • Competitive Clash — incredibly competitive, with a ranked system. Items found throughout the battlefield are no longer randomized and the battlefield pool (maps able to be played on) is shrunk to ensure balance. The top players in Competitive Clash receive huge rewards every season (with each season lasting a month). Provides a lot of XP and rewards upon victory. Friends can still compete against each other in this mode but there will be no rewards. Each player is given 90 seconds to make all of their actions per turn.

The Dark Dimension

Constant waves of enemies which repeatedly get stronger as the fight continues. Four types of this gamemode are available:

  • 10 Wave (Easy)
  • 50 Wave (Medium)
  • 100 Wave (Hard)
  • Endless (Insane)

This gamemode is way longer than normal battles and gives Champions who specialize in long fights their time to shine. Encourages teams with strong stat-stacking and/or utility. By the high/final waves, you'll be dealing with enemies which have ridiculous stats (like 3000 ATK/MAG). Gives great rewards for those willing to take the trek to the top (ranging from okay to amazing depending on the wave number). Top players in Endless mode will receive great rewards at the end of a season.

The Dark Dimension can be quit and resumed at any time (unless the season ends). A separate run of any given mode cannot be begun until the other run is won, lost or cancelled.

Crisis on Infinite Earths

Players unite into clans of up to 30 people. These clans participate in a battle which sees top characters from each clan ally themselves in order to defeat other clans. Clanmates will each select their best character to defend their Earth as they also provide support members for the character to receive stats from. The support characters will not participate in battle.

The defending characters will essentially be bosses for the enemy clan to face, with massive stats and partial infliction immunity. Each defending boss has its own set of immunities that vary per boss — complete stuns and immobilization effects are always blocked, but most other inflictions can still apply (giving Breakers and Plunderers a real role in offense). Clans are given 24 hours to setup their defense and 24 hours to attack, with each player having up to 5 attack attempts. The clan to successfully take the other Earth and have the highest score (most bosses defeated or cleared in better time) wins. Top-ranking clans at the end of a season will get amazing rewards.

Flashpoint

Incredibly speedy game mode which encourages early-fight dominators. Functions within the normal battle system with a few waves of enemies. Enemies will be frail but will move incredibly fast and hit very hard (around 1000-1800 ATK/MAG), encouraging evasion or high damage reduction. Once the waves are cleared (typically 3), great rewards are given (acting as a resource replenisher). Up to 3 attempts can be made per day but it can only be completed once per day.

Danger Room

Trials which test your understanding of a Champion's kit. Staged environments which require proper ability usage within a set amount of turns. Acts like a "Mastery" for understanding of the Champion. Provides rewards which buff the specific Champion (such as a strong artifact, EXP or extra stars).

✨ Status Effects

Status effects are temporary modifications applied to a Champion — whether by self, ally or enemy. They divide into positive effects (applications, beneficial to the Champion) and negative effects (inflictions, harmful to the Champion).

Non-stackable effects refresh their duration when reapplied; stackable effects accumulate. 📖 Term Definitions

Turns

Turns are acknowledged in various ways throughout the game. Many things such as cooldowns and status effects rely on turns so it's critical they're defined properly.

Turns can often be thought of like "active until all actions are chosen" for that many times. For example, a Champion could apply an ATK increasing status which lasts for 1 turn to themselves. That attack would provide a buff on their next rotation before vanishing (to give the character a chance to act before expiring). A status effect of 2 turns would apply on the following rotation as well. Negative effects apply in the same way, providing a negative effect for that many actions before expiring. Actions, as described here, don't account for extension abilities (which would count as an extension of the previous and thus would maintain the status effects which the previous action held).

If a status effect is damaging or disorienting (deals damage or impacts character choice), the effect of the status effect will apply at the start of a character's turn rather than waiting for the character to act first.

Cooldown Ignoring

Cooldown Ignoring lets a Champion use an ability that is still on cooldown by paying a cost, rather than waiting for the cooldown to elapse. The specific cost is defined by the granting kit — for example, Deadpool pays 6% max HP per remaining cooldown turn to fire an ability early.

A "cooldown turn ignored" refers to each turn of remaining cooldown on the ability at the moment it is used this way. An ability with 4 turns of cooldown remaining, used via Cooldown Ignoring, counts as 4 turns ignored. Abilities may scale with the number of cooldown turns ignored (e.g., "+X% per cooldown turn ignored"); these bonuses count the turns ignored on that specific use.

Cooldown Ignoring can be enabled "for free" by the Overdrive status (ignores cooldowns): while a Champion has Overdrive, they ignore cooldowns without paying the kit's cost. Note that "per cooldown turn ignored" scaling still counts the ability's attached cooldown number under Overdrive — the scaling is unaffected by the cost being waived.

After being used via Cooldown Ignoring, the ability returns to its base cooldown as normal; the ignored (banked) cooldown is consumed by the use and does not carry over.

Extension

Extensions are abilities tacked onto another ability at no extra cost. They function like a "combo" where it'll continue the first ability. These abilities are often represented by the phrase "extends into…" on a character's ability.

Standby

A standby is a reactionary state granted by certain abilities (typically marked with phrasing like "stands by for the remainder of the turn"). While standing by, the Champion takes no further actions but holds a triggered effect — usually a reactive benefit such as intercepting an incoming attack, saving an ally, or returning a counter.

Standby functions similarly to Block and Wait: the Champion stands by for the rest of the turn rather than continuing to act. Unlike those basic actions, the specific trigger and behavior of a standby is written into the ability that granted it, so each Champion's standby behaves differently. A standby's exit condition (one save, three counters, end of turn, etc.) is always specified in the granting ability.

Counter

A counter is a reactive attack that fires automatically in response to the Champion receiving an attack. Counters divide into two types:

  • Standby counter — a counter-attack triggered by a Standby state declared by a specific ability. The ability that grants the Standby defines exactly when and how the counter fires; it only applies while that Standby is active. When the Standby ends (turn expiry, usage limit reached, etc.), the counter expires with it.
  • Passive counter — a reactive counter-attack written permanently into a passive ability as an always-active or conditional response (e.g., "upon receiving an attack, deal X% ATK damage back to the attacker"). Unlike a Standby counter, no declared Standby state is required — the counter simply triggers whenever its stated condition is met.

Some abilities nullify all counters for the duration of their active window. When a counter is nullified, neither type fires — the attack is received without a reactive response. The nullifying ability will always state this explicitly (e.g., "nullifies any counters received while dashing").

Acting

A Champion is considered acting when they meet all of the following conditions:

  • Not currently in the Block state
  • Not currently in the Wait state
  • Not currently in a Standby state
  • Not affected by a movement- or action-restricting infliction: Stun, Frozen, Spider's Web, or an active Paralyze proc

Abilities that reference "while acting" condition their effect on this state. A passive or ability with an "acting" condition will not apply when the Champion is blocking, waiting, standing by, stunned, frozen, webbed, or currently unable to act due to a Paralyze proc firing that turn.

Last Stand

A Last Stand is a triggered effect that lets a Champion endure what would otherwise be a fatal blow, surviving the strike at 1 HP (or another value specified by the granting source). Last Stand is not a generic "survive" buff — it is gated by the exact condition written into the ability, costume, synergy, or passive that grants it.

Each Last Stand source specifies its own activation requirements and usage limit. Common patterns include "once per battle," "while above 50% HP at the start of the turn," or being keyed to a specific damage type. Unless an effect explicitly states otherwise, a Last Stand can be removed by enemy cleansing, prevented by infliction effects that suppress reactive abilities, or consumed without triggering if the activation condition isn't met when the fatal hit lands.

Taunt + Retribution Interaction

When Taunt (a positive effect on the defending Champion) and Retribution (a negative infliction on an enemy) are active simultaneously, the following priority rule applies:

  • Retribution takes precedence at any range. An enemy under Retribution must target the inflictor regardless of their position on the battlefield — Taunt's 6-panel radius restriction does not apply to that enemy.
  • Taunt still applies normally to all other enemies. Any enemy within 6 panels who is not under Retribution is still redirected to the Taunt holder as usual.

In short: Retribution is a range-unlimited forced target lock that overrides Taunt's proximity requirement for the specific enemy it affects. The two effects coexist without conflict — they simply address different enemies by different rules.

Overhealth

Overhealth is a temporary bonus layer of hit points applied on top of a character's HP pool. It can exceed maximum HP, and is only active for a set amount of time (specified by the kit).

Overhealth expires either when the duration is up or when it is depleted and cannot be healed like normal HP (unless a new application of overhealth occurs).

Elevation Bypass

Elevation Bypass is a passive modifier (referenced on Champion sheets as the tag Elevation Bypass) that removes restrictions related to elevation. It appears in two forms, and a Champion's kit will specify exactly which one applies:

  • "Ignores elevation differences when moving" — the movement-only variant. The Champion may ascend to any elevation without a valid access point, does not incur the extra STA cost for multi-floor descents, and may path through or over specific obstacles that call out Elevation Bypass compatibility (e.g., the Water Tower on Brooklyn Rooftops). Targeting rules are unaffected.
  • "Ignores elevation differences when targeting or moving" — the full variant. Includes everything above, and additionally removes elevation as a factor when selecting targets. The most notable implication: melee abilities (which would normally require the attacker and target to share an elevation tier) can reach targets at a different elevation — including airborne Champions — as though the height difference did not exist.

Both forms are represented by the Elevation Bypass tag on a Champion sheet. Read the kit wording to determine which variant is in effect. Elevation Bypass is not a status effect and cannot be removed by enemy abilities unless a kit explicitly states otherwise.
